## 2. Medications Review — Bedridden Patient

### Medications That Become MORE Important When Bedridden

| Medication | Why It Matters More |
|---|---|
| **Dabigatran** | Immobility significantly increases DVT (leg clot) and pulmonary embolism risk — now critical. Kidney function must be monitored as Dabigatran is renally cleared |
| **Vitamin D3** | No sun exposure at all — deficiency will accelerate |
| **Shelcal** | Bone loss speeds up dramatically with bed rest |
| **Eye drops** | Glaucoma doesn't pause. Lying flat raises eye pressure — monitoring may need to be more frequent |

### Medications That Must NOT Be Stopped Without Specialist Guidance

| Medication | Reason |
|---|---|
| **Syndopa Plus** | Parkinson's rigidity, tremor, and swallowing difficulties continue regardless of mobility. Abruptly stopping is dangerous — can cause severe withdrawal reaction. Dose timing becomes critical if swallowing is difficult |

### Medications Worth Reviewing With Doctor

| Medication | Discussion Point |
|---|---|
| **Silodol** | If patient has a urinary catheter, this may no longer be needed. If not catheterised, may still help prevent retention |

## 3. Causes of Continued Weakness

### From the Infection Itself

| Cause | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| **Post-infection fatigue** | Body expends enormous energy fighting infection — recovery exhaustion can last weeks |
| **Low oxygen during illness** | Even mild oxygen dips during chest infection cause muscle fatigue that lingers |
| **Poor nutrition/intake** | Reduced eating during illness depletes muscle fuel |
| **Dehydration** | Often underestimated — causes profound weakness, confusion, and low blood pressure |

### From Being Bedridden

| Cause | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| **Muscle deconditioning** | Muscles lose mass and strength rapidly — up to 5% per day in the first week of bed rest |
| **Orthostatic hypotension** | Blood pressure drops when sitting or being moved upright — especially common in Parkinson's |
| **Poor sleep quality** | Fragmented sleep in illness and bed rest doesn't restore properly |

### From Parkinson's Disease Specifically

| Cause | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| **Disease progression** | A significant physical stress like a bad infection can cause a step-down in Parkinson's function that doesn't fully recover |
| **Medication timing disruption** | If Syndopa Plus doses were missed or irregular during illness, motor function may still be catching up |
| **Dopamine and fatigue** | Parkinson's itself causes central fatigue independent of physical weakness |

### Blood Tests Worth Requesting

- Haemoglobin (anaemia)
- Sodium and potassium (electrolyte imbalances)
- Kidney function (especially important given Dabigatran use)
- Thyroid function
- Vitamin B12
- Vitamin D level
- Magnesium

## 4. Swallowing — SLT Assessment and Recovery

### Why Swallowing Is Difficult

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Parkinson's disease** | Directly impairs neurological coordination of swallowing — slow, weak, poorly timed muscle movements |
| **5 months of NG tube feeding** | Swallowing muscles have had almost no use — significant disuse atrophy |
| **Aspiration pneumonia recovery** | Throat and airway inflamed; cough reflex may be weakened |
| **85 years of age** | Muscle recovery is slower; neurological plasticity is reduced |
| **Prolonged bed rest** | General weakness reduces strength available for swallowing |
| **Fatigue** | Swallowing requires concentration and coordination — fatigue makes it unsafe |

### What Is an SLT Assessment?

**SLT = Speech and Language Therapist.** Despite the name, swallowing rehabilitation is one of their primary specialties.

| Assessment Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **Bedside clinical assessment** | Therapist observes alertness, posture, mouth movement, tongue strength, cough reflex, voice quality |
| **Trial swallows** | Small amounts of water or thickened liquid given carefully while therapist watches for signs of aspiration |
| **FEES** (Flexible Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing) | Thin camera passed through the nose to directly watch food/liquid passing through the throat — gold standard, can be done at bedside |
| **VFSS** (Videofluoroscopic Swallowing Study) | X-ray video of swallowing in real time — shows exactly where material enters the airway |

> FEES is most practical for a bedridden patient — no need to move to radiology.

### Swallowing Recovery Plan

#### Phase 1 — Safety First (Weeks 1–4)
Goal is not eating yet — rebuilding basic mechanics safely.

| Activity | How Often | Who |
|---|---|---|
| **Oral hygiene** | Every 4–6 hours | Family/carer — critical to reduce bacteria causing aspiration pneumonia |
| **Oral stimulation** | 2–3x daily | Wet sponge swab on gums, tongue, inside cheeks — wakes up mouth's sensory system |
| **Ice chip trials** | Under SLT guidance only | Tiny amounts of ice melt slowly — low volume, stimulates swallowing reflex |
| **Upright positioning** | All therapy sessions | Minimum 60–90 degrees, head slightly forward (chin tuck) |

#### Phase 2 — Swallowing Exercises (Ongoing, Under SLT)

| Exercise | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Tongue push-ups** | Press tongue hard against roof of mouth, hold 5 seconds — builds tongue strength |
| **Effortful swallow** | Swallow saliva while squeezing all throat muscles hard — strengthens pharyngeal muscles |
| **Chin tuck against resistance** | Press chin down against pillow or hand — strengthens muscles that protect the airway |
| **Mendelsohn maneuver** | Hold throat elevated at peak of swallow — improves airway closure timing |
| **Shaker exercise (modified)** | Lift only head to look at toes, hold — strengthens muscles that open the food passage |

#### Phase 3 — Gradual Oral Introduction (Only When SLT Clears It)

| Stage | What Is Given |
|---|---|
| **Puree/minced** | Smooth yogurt, pureed dal, mashed banana — nothing that crumbles or has lumps |
| **Thickened liquids** | Water thickened to honey or nectar consistency — flows slower, easier to control |
| **Soft foods** | Soft-cooked vegetables, custard — only after puree is tolerated safely |

> NG tube continues during this phase — oral intake supplements, not replaces, tube feeding until intake is consistently safe and adequate.

### Key Rules During Swallowing Therapy

- Always sit at 90 degrees when eating or drinking — never lying flat
- Time meals with Syndopa Plus peak effect (usually 45–60 minutes after dose)
- Small frequent meals — less fatigue per meal than three large ones
- Rigorous oral hygiene — bacteria in the mouth cause aspiration pneumonia

### Realistic Timeline

| Goal | Timeframe |
|---|---|
| Safe oral intake of small amounts (puree/thickened) alongside tube feeding | 6–12 weeks with consistent therapy |
| Partial return to oral nutrition reducing tube dependence | Possible over 3–6 months |
| Full removal of NG tube | Uncertain — depends on Parkinson's progression |
| Improved strength and sitting tolerance | 4–8 weeks with daily physiotherapy and good nutrition |

### Warning Signs of Aspiration

Coughing or choking during or after eating, wet or gurgly voice after swallowing, or recurrent chest infections — these are signs of silent aspiration and need urgent SLT assessment.

### Note on Long-Term NG Tube Use

After 5 months, a Ryle's tube was designed for short-term use. Ask the doctor about a **PEG tube** (placed directly into the stomach through the abdomen) — more comfortable for long-term feeding, doesn't affect the nose or throat, and doesn't interfere with swallowing therapy.

## 5. Medication Interactions

### CRITICAL — Dabigatran Cannot Be Given Via NG Tube

| Problem | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Dabigatran capsules must never be opened or crushed** | The pellets depend on their coating — opening them causes unpredictable, dangerous blood levels |
| **No approved liquid formulation exists** | There is no safe way to administer Dabigatran through an NG tube |
| **What should happen** | Switch to **Rivaroxaban or Apixaban** (tablets can be crushed for NG tube) or **injectable LMWH (Enoxaparin)** |

> **Raise this urgently with the treating doctor if not already addressed.**

---

### Significant Interactions

#### 1. Silodosin + Dabigatran — Increased Bleeding Risk

| Detail | |
|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Silodosin inhibits P-glycoprotein (P-gp), a transporter that clears Dabigatran from the body |
| **Effect** | Higher Dabigatran blood levels than intended — increased bleeding risk |
| **Signs to watch** | Unusual bruising, blood in urine or stool, prolonged bleeding from small cuts |

#### 2. Multiple Medications Lowering Blood Pressure

| Medication | How It Lowers BP |
|---|---|
| **Levodopa (Syndopa Plus)** | Causes orthostatic hypotension — well known in Parkinson's |
| **Silodosin** | Alpha-blocker — relaxes blood vessels |
| **Brimonidine (in Simbrinza)** | Systemic absorption from eye drops — lowers BP and heart rate |
| **Timolol (in Ganfort)** | Beta-blocker absorbed systemically from eye drops |

> Combined effect: weakness, fainting, reduced consciousness — may be contributing significantly to the patient's current weakness.

#### 3. Levodopa + Tube Feed Protein — Reduced Absorption

| Detail | |
|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Levodopa competes with amino acids from protein in feed for gut absorption |
| **Effect** | Unpredictable motor control if feed is running during dose |
| **What to do** | **Pause tube feed 30–60 minutes before and after each Syndopa Plus dose** |

---

### Moderate Interactions

| Interaction | Action |
|---|---|
| **Shelcal (Calcium) + Levodopa** | Separate by at least 1–2 hours |
| **Zincovit (B6) + Levodopa** | B6 can reduce levodopa effectiveness — check B6 dose in Zincovit. Carbidopa mitigates this partially |
| **Shelcal + Zincovit (Calcium + Zinc)** | Compete for absorption — separate by 1–2 hours |
| **Vitamin D double dose** | Patient is on weekly D3 AND Shelcal which contains D3 — confirm total dose with doctor |

---

### Eye Drops — Systemic Absorption

Eye drops in elderly patients are absorbed systemically more than in younger people via nasolacrimal drainage.

| Eye Drop | Systemic Effect |
|---|---|
| **Timolol (Ganfort)** | Bradycardia, fatigue, worsening of breathing, hypotension — can worsen weakness significantly |
| **Brimonidine (Simbrinza)** | Sedation, fatigue, low blood pressure, dry mouth — CNS depression |

**Technique to reduce systemic absorption:** After each drop, press the inner corner of the eye (near nose) for 1–2 minutes — blocks nasolacrimal duct.

---

### Interaction Summary — Priority Table

| Priority | Issue | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Urgent** | Dabigatran via NG tube is unsafe | Discuss alternative anticoagulant with doctor immediately |
| **High** | Silodosin + Dabigatran bleeding risk | Doctor to review both |
| **High** | Levodopa timing around tube feed | Pause feed 30–60 min before and after each Syndopa dose |
| **High** | Multiple BP-lowering medications causing weakness | Doctor to review eye drops and Silodosin |
| **Medium** | Calcium and Levodopa timing | Separate by 1–2 hours |
| **Medium** | Calcium and Zinc taken together | Separate by 1–2 hours |
| **Low** | Vitamin D total dose | Confirm with doctor |
| **Practical** | Eye drop systemic absorption | Use nasolacrimal occlusion after each drop |

## 6. Medications to Avoid and Add

### NEVER Give — Can Severely Worsen Parkinson's

These block dopamine and can cause dramatic, sometimes irreversible worsening. All carers must know these by name.

| Medication | Why Dangerous | Commonly Given For |
|---|---|---|
| **Metoclopramide (Perinorm, Reglan)** | Blocks dopamine — severe rigidity, crisis | Nausea, vomiting, gastric emptying |
| **Haloperidol** | Strong dopamine blocker — catastrophic in Parkinson's | Agitation, delirium, hiccups |
| **Prochlorperazine (Stemetil)** | Dopamine blocker | Nausea, dizziness, vertigo |
| **Olanzapine, Risperidone** | Dopamine blockers despite being "atypical" | Agitation, sleep, psychiatric symptoms |
| **Chlorpromazine** | Strong dopamine blocker | Nausea, hiccups, agitation |

> If antipsychotic is absolutely needed: only **Quetiapine (low dose)** or **Clozapine** are relatively safer in Parkinson's.
> For nausea: **Domperidone** is the only safe alternative to Metoclopramide.

---

### Avoid — Dangerous in Frail Elderly (Beers Criteria)

| Category | Examples | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **First-generation antihistamines** | Chlorpheniramine, Diphenhydramine, Promethazine | Confusion, sedation, urinary retention |
| **Benzodiazepines** | Diazepam, Lorazepam, Alprazolam | Respiratory depression — dangerous post-pneumonia |
| **Tricyclic antidepressants** | Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline | Anticholinergic effects, cardiac arrhythmia |
| **Strong anticholinergics** | Oxybutynin, Hyoscine | Confusion, constipation, urinary retention |
| **Muscle relaxants** | Baclofen, Tizanidine | Sedation, respiratory depression |
| **Tramadol** | Tramadol | Seizure risk, serotonin interaction, confusion |

> **For pain relief:** Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) via NG tube is the safe option.

---

### Avoid — Increase Bleeding Risk with Dabigatran

| Medication | Risk |
|---|---|
| **NSAIDs** (Ibuprofen, Diclofenac, Naproxen) | Significantly increase bleeding — avoid completely |
| **Aspirin** | Adds to bleeding risk unless specifically prescribed for cardiac reason |
| **Fluoroquinolone antibiotics** (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin) | Interact with Dabigatran — flag to doctor if antibiotics prescribed |

---

### Medications to Consider Adding — Discuss With Doctor

#### Almost Certainly Needed

| Medication | Why |
|---|---|
| **Laxative (Lactulose or Macrogol/Movicol)** | Constipation is universal in Parkinson's — worsened by bed rest, tube feeding, and immobility. Unmanaged constipation reduces feed tolerance and increases aspiration risk |
| **Domperidone** | Improves gastric emptying (gastroparesis is common in Parkinson's), reduces nausea, only safe pro-motility drug in Parkinson's |
| **Safer anticoagulant** | Replace Dabigatran — Apixaban (crushable) or injectable Enoxaparin for NG tube administration |

#### Strongly Worth Reviewing

| Medication | Why |
|---|---|
| **Proton Pump Inhibitor** (Pantoprazole or Omeprazole) | Bedridden NG-tube patients are at high risk for stress gastric ulcers |
| **Vitamin B12** | 5 months of tube feeding in an 85-year-old — B12 deficiency causes profound weakness, neuropathy, and confusion. Simple blood test to check |
| **Mucolytic** (Carbocisteine or N-Acetylcysteine) | Thins secretions post-pneumonia — easier to clear, reduces re-infection risk |
| **Magnesium supplement** | Commonly low in elderly tube-fed patients — causes cramps, weakness, irregular heart rhythm |
| **Antidepressant** (Sertraline, low dose) | Depression affects up to 50% of Parkinson's patients — manifests as withdrawal, reduced motivation, worsens all rehabilitation |

---

### Anticholinergic Burden Review

Multiple medications with mild anticholinergic effects add up — cumulative effect causes confusion, constipation, urinary retention, dry mouth (worsens swallowing), and blurred vision.

> Ask the doctor to perform an **ACB (Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden) score** review across all current medications.

---

### Non-Medication Additions

| Addition | Why |
|---|---|
| **Compression stockings or pneumatic compression** | DVT prevention alongside anticoagulation — immobility is a major clot risk |
| **Pressure ulcer assessment (Braden scale)** | This patient is very high risk — urgent if not already being assessed |
| **Regular blood tests** | Renal function, B12, Magnesium, Haemoglobin, Vitamin D — cheap and could explain much of the weakness |
| **High-protein tube feed review** | Standard tube feeds may lack adequate protein — target 1.2–1.5g protein/kg/day for muscle maintenance |

## 7. CBD — Over the Counter Options

### Legal and Availability Status in India

| Status | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Hemp-derived CBD (under 0.3% THC)** | Legal in India under FSSAI guidelines as a food supplement — available without prescription |
| **Available in Delhi** | Yes — several brands sell online and in select wellness/pharmacy stores |
| **Medical CBD (prescription)** | Not yet widely established in India but evolving |
| **Quality control** | Variable — product labeling and actual concentration may not always match. Stick to established brands |

---

### Potential Benefits for This Patient

| Condition | How CBD May Help | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Parkinson's — rigidity and tremor** | May reduce muscle stiffness and tremor through endocannabinoid system | Moderate — small human studies show modest benefit |
| **Parkinson's — sleep disturbance** | Improves sleep quality and reduces REM sleep behaviour disorder common in Parkinson's | Reasonable evidence |
| **Parkinson's — anxiety and agitation** | Calming effect without blocking dopamine — safe unlike antipsychotics | Good evidence |
| **Pain from prolonged bed rest** | Anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties — joint and muscle pain relief | Good evidence |
| **Post-pneumonia inflammation** | Reduces residual lung inflammation | Early but promising evidence |
| **General neuroprotection** | Some evidence of slowing neurodegeneration in Parkinson's animal models | Preliminary |

---

### Important Concerns for This Specific Patient

#### 1. GLAUCOMA — Caution

| Finding | Detail |
|---|---|
| **THC lowers intraocular pressure** | Well established — but CBD is not THC |
| **CBD may slightly RAISE eye pressure** | Some studies show CBD can counteract the IOP-lowering effect of THC and may independently raise IOP in some patients |
| **Patient is already on three glaucoma drops** | Adding something that may raise IOP is a risk |
| **Recommendation** | Discuss specifically with the ophthalmologist before using any CBD product — avoid products containing any THC |

#### 2. CBD + Dabigatran — Bleeding Risk

| Detail | |
|---|---|
| **CBD inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP2C9 enzymes** | These enzymes help break down Dabigatran |
| **Effect** | CBD can raise Dabigatran blood levels — increasing bleeding risk |
| **Recommendation** | Only consider oral CBD if anticoagulation is under close medical monitoring |

#### 3. CBD + Levodopa (Syndopa Plus)

| Detail | |
|---|---|
| **Generally considered compatible** | CBD does not block dopamine pathways |
| **May complement levodopa** | Reduces dyskinesias (involuntary movements) in some Parkinson's patients on long-term levodopa |
| **Timing** | Separate from Syndopa Plus by 1–2 hours as a precaution |

---

### Safest Form for This Patient

Given the NG tube, drug interactions, and frailty — topical CBD is the safest starting point:

| Form | Suitability | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **CBD topical cream or oil** | Best first option | Applied to skin — no systemic drug interactions, no effect on Dabigatran or glaucoma, good for local muscle rigidity and joint pain |
| **CBD oil (sublingual/NG tube)** | Only with doctor's approval | Has systemic effects — benefits are greater but so are interaction risks |
| **CBD capsules** | Only with doctor's approval | Same as above |
| **CBD gummies** | Not suitable | Cannot be given via NG tube |

## 8. Home Care Routines

### 8.\1 Positioning and Pressure Ulcer Prevention

This is the single most important physical care routine. Pressure sores can develop within 2–4 hours on a bony area.

| Task | How Often | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Reposition patient** | Every 2 hours, day and night | Turn from back to left side to right side — use a schedule |
| **Check skin at bony points** | Every repositioning | Heels, sacrum (tailbone), hips, shoulder blades, back of head, ankles — look for redness that doesn't fade when pressed |
| **Keep skin clean and dry** | After every incontinence episode | Moisture causes rapid skin breakdown |
| **Pressure-relieving mattress** | Continuously | Air alternating pressure mattress or high-density foam — standard mattresses are inadequate |
| **Heel protection** | Always | Heel protector boots or pillows under calves to float heels off mattress |
| **Head of bed elevation** | Always | 30–45 degrees — reduces aspiration risk and pressure on sacrum |

> **Early warning signs of pressure sore:** Persistent redness, warmth, hardness, or darkening of skin — needs immediate attention before skin breaks open.

---

### 8.\1 NG Tube Care

| Task | How Often | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Check tube position** | Before every feed | Use pH paper on aspirate — should be acidic (pH under 5.5). Never feed if position uncertain |
| **Check tube is secure** | Every shift | Tape intact, not pulling on nostril — change if loose or skin irritated |
| **Flush tube** | Before and after every feed and medication | 30–50ml cooled boiled water — prevents blockages |
| **Check nostril skin** | Daily | Pressure sores can develop at nostril — reposition tape regularly |
| **Oral care** | Every 4–6 hours | Mouth collects bacteria even without oral intake |
| **Monitor abdomen** | Before feeds | Distension or vomiting means stomach not emptying — pause feed, inform doctor |
| **Check residual volume** | Before each bolus feed | If more than 200ml remains, delay feed and inform doctor |

> Never put patient flat during or for 1 hour after tube feeding.

---

### 8.\1 Oral Care — Critical for Aspiration Pneumonia Prevention

Bacteria in the mouth cause lung damage when secretions are aspirated.

| Task | How Often | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Brush teeth or clean gums** | Morning and night | Soft toothbrush or foam swab — clean gums and tongue even without teeth |
| **Oral swabbing** | Every 4–6 hours | Damp foam swab around all mouth surfaces — remove pooled secretions |
| **Lip moisturiser** | 3–4 times daily | Cracked lips are painful and harbour bacteria |
| **Suction if needed** | As needed | Manual or electric suction device at home if patient cannot clear secretions |
| **Swallowing exercises** | 2–3 times daily | Under SLT guidance — see Section 4 |

---

### 8.\1 Eye Medication Routine

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Wait 5 minutes between each drop** | Each drop needs time to absorb before the next |
| **Recommended order** | Systane (tears) → Simbrinza → Netalo → Ganfort — 5 minutes between each |
| **Nasolacrimal occlusion** | After each drop, press inner corner of eye for 1–2 minutes — reduces systemic absorption |
| **Clean eye area first** | Gently wipe away discharge before applying drops |
| **Check eyes daily** | Redness, discharge, cloudiness, swelling — report to ophthalmologist |
| **Discard opened bottles after 28 days** | Even if liquid remains — contamination risk |

---

### 8.\1 Medication Administration Via NG Tube

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Each medication separately** | Never mix medications in the tube |
| **Flush between each medication** | 10–15ml water between each drug |
| **Check which tablets can be crushed** | Confirm with pharmacist for each one. Dabigatran must NOT be crushed |
| **Syndopa Plus timing** | Pause tube feed 30–60 minutes before and after each dose |
| **Never delay Syndopa Plus** | Set alarms — even 30-minute delays cause noticeable stiffness |
| **Keep a medication log** | Record exact time of each dose — helps identify if weakness correlates with missed doses |

---

### 8.\1 Bowel and Bladder Care

| Task | How Often | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Bowel movement tracking** | Daily record | If no movement for 2–3 days, laxative dose may need adjustment |
| **Abdominal massage** | Daily | Gentle clockwise circular massage around abdomen stimulates bowel |
| **Catheter care (if present)** | Daily | Clean around entry point, bag below bladder level, empty when two-thirds full, check for cloudiness or odour |
| **If no catheter** | Watch for retention | Distended abdomen, restlessness, no urine in 8+ hours — needs medical attention |
| **Skin care after incontinence** | Immediately | Clean with warm water, pat dry, apply barrier cream — never rub |

---

### 8.\1 Physiotherapy Routine at Home

| Exercise | How Often | How To Do It |
|---|---|---|
| **Passive limb movements** | Twice daily, 10–15 minutes | Gently move each joint — ankles (circles), knees (bend/straighten), hips, shoulders, elbows, wrists |
| **Ankle pumps** | Every 2 hours while awake | Push foot up and down repeatedly — prevents DVT and swelling |
| **Sitting up in bed** | Twice daily if tolerated | Supported sitting for 20–30 minutes improves lung function and alertness |
| **Hand and finger movement** | Daily | Open and close fists, stretch fingers — reduces stiffness |
| **Chest physiotherapy** | If secretions present | Gentle cupped-hand tapping on back while patient on side — loosens secretions |

---

### 8.\1 Nutrition and Tube Feeding

| Task | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Feed position** | Head of bed at 30–45 degrees minimum during and for 1 hour after feeding |
| **Feed temperature** | Room temperature — cold feeds cause cramping |
| **Hydration** | Ensure adequate water flushes in addition to feed |
| **Watch for feed intolerance** | Vomiting, bloating, diarrhoea, large residual volumes — adjust rate or formula |
| **Weigh patient monthly** | Weight loss signals inadequate nutrition despite tube feeding |

---

### 8.\1 Respiratory Care

| Task | How Often | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Positioning for lung drainage** | Built into repositioning schedule | Side positions naturally drain different lung segments |
| **Deep breathing** | Hourly while awake | 5–10 deep breaths if patient can cooperate — manual chest physio if not |
| **Watch for infection signs** | Daily | Increased secretions, colour change (yellow/green), fever, faster breathing, reduced alertness — seek medical attention |
| **Humidify the room** | Continuously | Dry air thickens secretions — room humidifier especially important at night |
| **Head never fully flat** | Always | Slight elevation protects the airway at all times |

---

### 8.\10 Mental and Emotional Wellbeing

| Approach | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Talk to the patient** | Even if response is limited — familiar voices provide comfort and orientation. Always assume the patient can hear and understand |
| **Maintain a routine** | Same times for feeds, medications, care — predictability reduces agitation and anxiety |
| **Sensory stimulation** | Music the patient enjoyed, radio, family voices — prevents sensory deprivation |
| **Explain before doing** | Tell patient what you are about to do before each care task — reduces startle and distress |
| **Manage pain actively** | Restlessness, grimacing, unusual stiffness may be unspoken pain — Paracetamol via tube is safe and often underprescribed |
| **Carer wellbeing** | Fatigue in carers leads to errors — share shifts, take rest, seek outside support |

---

### 8.\11 When to Call the Doctor Immediately

| Sign | Possible Cause |
|---|---|
| Fever above 38°C | Infection — chest, urinary, or pressure sore |
| Increased or laboured breathing | Aspiration, pneumonia, heart failure |
| Green or yellow secretions increasing | Chest infection starting |
| Sudden increase in rigidity or freezing | Syndopa timing issue or serious illness |
| Bleeding from any site | Dabigatran-related — urgent review |
| No urine output for 8+ hours | Urinary retention or severe dehydration |
| Unresponsive or very difficult to rouse | Medical emergency — call emergency services |
| Vomiting with tube in place | Sit upright, stop feed, call doctor — aspiration risk |
| Tube comes out or position uncertain | Stop all feeds immediately — do not re-feed until position medically confirmed |

### 8.\12 Daily Care Schedule

| Time | Task |
|---|---|
| **Morning** | Oral care, eye drops, medications via tube, passive physiotherapy, reposition, skin check, tube feed |
| **Mid-morning** | Reposition, ankle pumps, swallowing exercises, check bowel |
| **Afternoon** | Tube feed, medications, sit upright if tolerated, music or conversation, skin check |
| **Evening** | Oral care, eye drops, medications, passive physiotherapy, reposition |
| **Night** | Reposition every 2 hours, maintain head elevation, monitor breathing |

## Priority Checklist for Doctor Visit

| Priority | Topic |
|---|---|
